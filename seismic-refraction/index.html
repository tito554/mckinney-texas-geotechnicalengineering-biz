The contrast between a site in historic McKinney near the Cottonwood Creek floodplain and one perched on the Austin Chalk outcrops east of US-75 can be night and day. One might show 15 feet of soft alluvium over weathered shale; the other, competent limestone at six feet. In a city that added over 50,000 residents between 2010 and 2020, development pushes into areas where surface clues don't tell the full story. Seismic tomography cuts through the guesswork: we generate controlled wave energy at the surface and record arrival times across a geophone array, then invert travel-time data into a 2D or 3D velocity model. The result is a continuous image of stratigraphy, fracture zones, and rippability, which helps engineers avoid surprises when excavating for footings or planning deep excavations in mixed soil-rock profiles common across Collin County.

Tomographic inversion resolves hidden velocity layers that conventional refraction misses — critical where McKinney's weathered shale masks softer zones beneath.

Scope of work

On several McKinney projects east of Lake Forest Drive, we have observed that weathered shale and interbedded limestone lenses create velocity inversions that a simple refraction analysis would miss — the hidden low-velocity layer gets masked by the faster refractor above. That is precisely why we run tomographic inversion rather than relying on intercept-time methods alone. The technique handles lateral velocity gradients and hidden layers by iteratively ray-tracing through a starting model and minimizing residuals between observed and calculated travel times. For a typical 230-foot spread with 24 geophones at 10-foot spacing, we achieve a resolution of roughly 3 to 5 feet vertically, sufficient to delineate the top of competent bedrock within half a foot. When the project calls for correlating shear-wave velocity profiles at depth, we often pair seismic tomography with MASW to extract Vs30 for IBC seismic site classification, ensuring the velocity model serves both foundation design and earthquake engineering requirements.

Area-specific notes

McKinney's growth arc since the 1990s transformed farmland and pasture into subdivisions at a pace that occasionally outpaced subsurface due diligence. Older neighborhoods near Wilson Creek sit on expansive clay over weathered shale, while newer developments east toward Princeton encounter interbedded limestone and marl where dissolution features can lurk. Skipping a velocity model means accepting blind risk: a footing designed for uniform clay that actually bears on pinnacled rock will knowledge differential settlement, and a retention basin excavated into fractured chalk may leak into subsurface conduits. Seismic tomography identifies these transitions before the backhoe arrives, mapping the rock surface undulations that govern cut volumes and dewatering needs. For projects within the McKinney city limits, where the adopted IBC 2021 edition applies together with ASCE 7-22, the velocity cross-section also provides site-specific evidence to justify soil profile type selection rather than defaulting to the conservative — and costlier — Site Class D assumption.

Linked services

01

P-Wave Seismic Refraction Tomography

2D and 3D P-wave velocity models for mapping bedrock topography, rippability, and stratigraphic boundaries. Standard 24- or 48-channel spreads with sledgehammer or accelerated weight-drop source. Deliverables include ray-coverage plots, RMS error analysis, and depth-to-rock contours compatible with CAD and GIS platforms.

02

Seismic Reflection Profiling

High-resolution shallow reflection surveys for delineating sedimentary layering, fault offsets, and paleochannel geometry below 100 feet depth. Common-midpoint stacking with 48-channel acquisition, processed through bandpass filtering, deconvolution, and migration. Suitable for large-area site characterization and groundwater basin studies.

03

Vs30 and Site Classification Surveys

Combined P-wave refraction and MASW acquisition along the same spread to extract compressional and shear-wave velocity profiles. Used to assign IBC/ASCE 7 site class (A through F) for structural design. Meets the requirements of Section 20.4 of ASCE 7-22 for site-specific ground motion analysis.

04

Crosshole and Downhole Seismic Tomography

Borehole-to-borehole or surface-to-borehole velocity tomography for projects requiring the highest resolution at depth. Crosshole surveys achieve sub-foot resolution of inter-borehole velocity structure; ideal for critical infrastructure, bridge foundations, and seismic retrofit verification.

Quick answers

How deep can seismic refraction tomography see with a sledgehammer source in McKinney soils?

With a 230-foot spread and 24 geophones, a 16-pound sledgehammer on an aluminum strike plate typically provides a depth of investigation between 50 and 75 feet in the clay-over-shale conditions common across McKinney. For targets beyond 80 feet, we deploy an accelerated weight drop or use longer spreads with roll-along acquisition. The actual penetration depends on the velocity contrast and ambient noise — sites near active construction or SH-121 traffic may require stacking more shots to maintain signal quality.

What is the cost range for a seismic tomography survey in McKinney?

A typical refraction tomography survey with one or two spreads and standard processing runs between US$2,570 and US$5,110, depending on the lineal footage, number of shots, and whether we combine it with MASW for Vs30 profiling. Reflection surveys and crosshole tomography fall at the upper end due to acquisition density and processing complexity. Each proposal includes a fixed-price scope so you know the cost before mobilization.

Can seismic tomography detect underground voids or caves in limestone?

Yes, but with important caveats. Air-filled voids produce a strong velocity low that tomography can image if the void diameter is at least one-quarter of the depth and the geophone spacing is tight enough to sample the anomaly across several channels. In McKinney's Austin Chalk formation, dissolution features tend to be clay-filled rather than open — these still appear as velocity lows, though with less contrast. We recommend supplementing tomography with electrical resistivity profiling for ambiguous targets.

How does the McKinney building department accept seismic survey results for site classification?

The City of McKinney follows IBC 2021, which permits site-specific Vs30 determination by geophysical methods under Section 1613.3.2. We offer a signed report with the velocity cross-section, Vs30 profile, and the corresponding ASCE 7-22 site class. The report references ASTM D5777-18 acquisition procedures and includes the measured shear-wave velocity data so the reviewing engineer can verify the classification directly.
