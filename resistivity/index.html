McKinney grew fast—historic cotton hub turned booming Collin County city—and with that growth came subdivisions pushing east into the Blackland Prairie. The terrain here hides sandy clay interbeds and old creek channels that don't show up on a standard boring log. That is where an electrical resistivity survey becomes indispensable. Our lab team runs VES soundings with a 4‑electrode Schlumberger spread, reaching depths of 60 to 100 feet without turning a single auger. The resulting resistivity cross‑sections are paired with in‑situ permeability data when stormwater infiltration is part of the project, and we often tie the geoelectric layers to grain‑size curves from nearby borings to tighten the interpretation. The method works especially well in McKinney’s eastern expanses, where the Austin Chalk formation dips below the Taylor Marl and the contrast between clay and weathered limestone shows up clearly on a sounding curve.

A 100‑foot VES sounding can separate a perched water table from a true sand lens in less than an hour on site—information that would take three borings to confirm by drilling.

Scope of work

Collin County sits near the transition from the Blackland Prairie to the Eastern Cross Timbers. In McKinney, the upper 15 to 25 feet are typically stiff, dark‑gray Taylor Marl clays with a resistivity range of 2 to 8 ohm‑m, while the underlying weathered Austin Chalk can jump to 25–60 ohm‑m depending on fracture density and moisture. What we encounter most often is a perched water table at 8 to 12 feet after spring rains—this creates a low‑resistivity trough that is easy to misinterpret as a deeper clay layer if you do not have a calibrated local model. We run each VES sounding with a minimum of AB/2 increments from 1.5 meters out to 150 meters, and we invert the data using a smoothness‑constrained least‑squares routine. For projects where shallow bearing capacity is marginal, the resistivity profile helps place CPT soundings exactly where the clay‑chalk interface is steepest, saving time and reducing the number of required pushes. Because McKinney is in Seismic Design Category A per ASCE 7, the Vs30 parameter is not usually the driver—stratigraphy and shrink‑swell potential are the real concerns. We have seen electrical sections that map a paleochannel filled with sandy silt trending northeast under the Stonebridge Ranch area, a feature that directly affected mat foundation design for a three‑story medical office building.

Area-specific notes

A warehouse project on McKinney’s south side taught us a lesson worth sharing. The developer had a clean set of SPT borings spaced 200 feet apart—all showed stiff clay to 30 feet. During excavation, the contractor hit a water‑filled sand pocket that collapsed the north wall of the cut. Later, a single VES sounding run between two of the original borings showed a 15‑ohm‑m lens at 18 feet depth that the drilling had simply stepped over. In McKinney’s Taylor Marl terrain, these paleochannel remnants are more common than the geologic maps suggest. Skipping a resistivity survey when the project covers more than an acre means accepting a blind spot between borings. The risk is not just groundwater—it is differential settlement if a shallow footing straddles a sharp change from high‑plasticity clay to silty sand. Our lab addresses this by integrating resistivity with in‑situ permeability testing and using the combined data to draw a continuous stratigraphic fence that engineers can actually design from.

Linked services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

Schlumberger array with AB/2 spacing up to 150 m. We deliver a layered resistivity model, a sounding curve with RMS error, and a geoelectric column tied to local stratigraphy—ideal for foundation depth studies and shrink‑swell assessment.

02

2D Resistivity Imaging (ERT)

Multi‑electrode Wenner‑Schlumberger profiles using 56 or 112 electrodes. Used for mapping lateral changes across McKinney’s paleochannels and for locating the Austin Chalk surface beneath commercial building pads.

03

Soil Resistivity for Grounding Design

Wenner 4‑pin method at spacings of 5, 10, 20, 40, and 80 feet per IEEE 81. We offer apparent resistivity versus electrode spacing, two‑layer soil model parameters, and a report formatted for substation or tower grounding analysis.

Quick answers

What does an electrical resistivity survey in McKinney typically cost?

For a standard 1D VES sounding reaching 60 to 100 feet of investigation depth, the cost ranges from US$670 to US$930 per sounding, depending on access conditions and the number of AB/2 increments required. A 2D profile with 56 electrodes generally falls between US$1,800 and US$2,600. Each quote includes data processing, inversion, and a signed report from our accredited lab.

How does resistivity testing handle McKinney's expansive clay soils?

Expansive clays like the Taylor Marl show very low resistivity—usually 2 to 6 ohm‑m—because they hold water tightly and have high cation exchange capacity. The VES sounding detects the thickness of this clay layer and identifies whether it transitions into chalk or a sandier unit below. We calibrate the resistivity boundaries with Atterberg limits from nearby borings so the geotechnical engineer can compute potential vertical rise with more confidence in the layer depths.

Can you run a resistivity line in a developed neighborhood with underground utilities?

Yes, and we do it regularly in McKinney’s older subdivisions. We call in a private utility locate and use a Wenner array oriented parallel to known utility runs to minimize interference. If metallic pipes are unavoidable, we note their position and invert the data with a topography and metal‑noise filter. The resulting section is still reliable for mapping the clay‑chalk contact, though the shallowest 5 feet may show distortion from backfill and utility trenches.
